Cape Town is the kind of city that makes people change their plans. Travellers who book three nights end up staying ten. Those who come for the wine region find themselves obsessed with the mountains, or the penguins, or the street food in Woodstock. The city sits at the meeting point of two oceans, beneath one of the world's most recognisable mountains, and manages to combine natural spectacle with a rich, complex cultural life in a way that very few destinations can match. A week is the minimum that does it justice — here is how to spend those seven days well.

## Days 1–2: City Bowl and the Atlantic Seaboard

Start where most visitors start: Table Mountain. Take the revolving cable car to the summit early — before 9am if possible — to beat the queues and the cloud that often rolls in by midday. The views extend across the Cape Peninsula in one direction and over the city and harbour in the other. Allow 90 minutes on top, and consider the short walk to the lip of the western face for the most dramatic perspectives.

The Bo-Kaap neighbourhood, just below Signal Hill on the fringes of the City Bowl, is one of Cape Town's most photographed areas — a steep hillside of brightly painted houses that was historically the heart of the city's Cape Malay community. Come for the colour and the atmosphere, but also for the food: Bo-Kaap Kombuis and Biesmiellah are two of the oldest Cape Malay restaurants in the city, serving slow-cooked curries, pickled fish, and koeksisters (syrup-soaked fried dough) at prices that feel generous given the quality.

On your second day, head to the V&A Waterfront for a morning, then make the 45-minute drive down to Boulders Beach near Simon's Town to visit the resident colony of African penguins. The penguins are wild but completely habituated to human presence, and watching them waddle through the boulders and body-surf small waves in the clear blue water is genuinely charming. Book the entry online to avoid queues.

## Days 3–4: The Cape Peninsula

The Cape Peninsula drive is one of the great scenic routes in the world and deserves a full day. Head south along the Atlantic side via Camps Bay and Llandudno — the beaches here are beautiful but cold, fed by the Benguela Current from the Atlantic. Chapman's Peak Drive, a winding coastal road blasted into the cliffs between Hout Bay and Noordhoek, offers some of the most dramatic coastal scenery in Africa. Stop at the viewpoint halfway along and take your time.

Cape Point, at the southern tip of the Peninsula, sits within the Table Mountain National Park. The rocky headland isn't technically the southernmost point of Africa (that's Cape Agulhas, two hours east) but it feels like the end of the world. Walk up to the old lighthouse for views back along the Peninsula and out to sea. Look for Cape mountain zebra and ostriches in the fynbos on the drive in.

On day four, explore at a slower pace. Hout Bay's Mariner's Wharf is touristy but the fish and chips are genuinely excellent. Kalk Bay, further around the False Bay side of the Peninsula, is a fishing village turned arts enclave with great antique shops, the acclaimed Olympia Café for breakfast, and a working harbour where fishermen sell their catch from the dock.

## Days 5–6: The Cape Winelands

Stellenbosch, an hour's drive from Cape Town through wheat fields and vineyards, is the heart of the Cape Winelands and one of the most handsome towns in South Africa. The town centre is built around oak-lined streets of Dutch and Georgian architecture, and the university gives it an energetic café culture. Wine estates ring the town in every direction; Rust en Vrede, Vergelegen, and Waterford are among the most acclaimed, and most offer tastings for around R150–R200 (roughly $8–$11) per person.

Franschhoek, 30km east of Stellenbosch through the Helshoogte Pass, is the country's self-declared food capital. The main street is lined with restaurants drawing on French Huguenot traditions — the village was established by French Protestant refugees in the late 17th century. Book a table at The Tasting Room at Le Quartier Français in advance if your budget allows; for something more casual, the courtyard restaurants along Huguenot Street are excellent. A glass of local Chenin Blanc in the afternoon sun here is hard to beat.

Cape Malay cuisine deserves a special mention across both days in the Winelands. Look for bobotie (spiced mince topped with egg custard), bredie (slow-cooked lamb and tomato stew), and the extraordinary variety of spiced rice dishes that trace their roots to Indonesian and Malaysian cooking brought to the Cape by enslaved people in the 17th and 18th centuries.

## Day 7: Neighbourhoods and a Farewell Sunset

Spend your final day in the city's most interesting neighbourhoods. Woodstock, east of the City Bowl, has been Cape Town's street art district for over a decade. Albert Road and the streets around it are lined with large-scale murals, and the neighbourhood is also home to independent studios, design shops, and the Old Biscuit Mill. The Mill's Saturday market (Neighbourgoods Market) is one of the best food and craft markets in Africa — arrive before 11am for the best pickings.

De Waterkant, the small Cape Dutch neighbourhood between the City Bowl and the V&A Waterfront, is worth an afternoon wander. It's compact, walkable, and full of good coffee, independent boutiques, and the slightly lost-in-time atmosphere of old Cape Town architecture surviving into the present.

For your farewell sunset, Signal Hill offers the best vantage point in the city. Drive or walk up in the late afternoon, bring a bottle of local wine, and watch the sun drop into the Atlantic behind Robben Island. It costs nothing and is one of the finest views the city offers.

## Practical Tips

- **Best time to visit:** November to March is summer in Cape Town, with long days, warm temperatures, and the wine harvest season in February–March. April and May are beautiful and less crowded. July and August can be rainy and cold but are the best time for whale watching at Hermanus.
- **Safety basics:** Cape Town requires sensible awareness. Stick to well-trafficked areas at night, use Uber or Bolt rather than walking after dark in unfamiliar neighbourhoods, and follow local advice on specific areas to avoid. The tourist-facing areas — Waterfront, City Bowl, the Winelands — are generally safe during the day.
- **Getting around:** A hire car is strongly recommended for the Peninsula drive and Winelands days. Uber and Bolt work well within the city. The MyCiTi bus network connects the City Bowl, Atlantic Seaboard, and Airport efficiently and cheaply.
- **Currency:** The South African rand offers very favourable exchange rates for most international visitors, making Cape Town excellent value for dining, accommodation, and wine tasting.
