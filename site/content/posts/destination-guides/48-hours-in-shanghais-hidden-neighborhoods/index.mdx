Shanghai is two cities sharing one address. On one side: the Pudong skyline, all glass towers and elevated expressways, a monument to the speed and ambition of twenty-first century urban development. On the other: the lilongmen alleyways, plane-tree-lined boulevards, and Art Deco facades of a city that absorbed French colonial influence, Soviet brutalism, and traditional Jiangnan architecture and somehow made them cohere. Most first-time visitors spend their limited time on the obvious sights and leave feeling like they've seen a highlight reel without the film. This 48-hour itinerary is designed to correct that — to push past the famous landmarks and into the neighbourhoods where Shanghai's real texture lives.

Forty-eight hours is tight, but it is enough to get a genuine sense of the city if you move with intention and resist the temptation to over-schedule.

## Day 1: The Bund, French Concession, and Xintiandi

Start at the Bund early — before 8am if you can manage it. The riverside promenade facing the Pudong skyline is genuinely one of the world's great urban views, and experiencing it without tour groups pressing in from every angle transforms it entirely. The neoclassical banking houses and trading company buildings lining the western side of the promenade are worth a slow look: these are the buildings that made Shanghai the commercial capital of the Far East in the early twentieth century, and the architecture communicates power and ambition at a scale few cities match.

From the Bund, walk or take the metro to the French Concession for mid-morning. The area centred on Wukang Road and Anfu Road is one of the most walkable neighbourhoods in Asia — a succession of independent cafes, concept stores, and architectural surprises tucked behind wrought-iron gates and trailing wisteria. This is not a museum district; people live and work here, and the neighbourhood's appeal comes precisely from that everyday quality. Stop for coffee at one of the dozen or so specialty roasters that have opened here in the past few years.

Xintiandi, a short walk or metro stop from the heart of the French Concession, represents a controversial but undeniably impressive piece of urban regeneration. A cluster of Shikumen (stone-gate house) buildings — the characteristic two-storey brick residential architecture of old Shanghai — has been preserved and converted into restaurants, bars, and cultural spaces around a pedestrianised plaza. The architecture is genuine even if the commercial programme around it is polished. The Shanghai History Museum on the edge of the complex is worth an hour and provides essential context for everything you'll see in the two days ahead.

Spend the evening back on the Bund or at one of the riverside bar terraces, watching the Pudong towers light up across the water. The light show is free, nightly, and spectacular.

## Day 2: Yu Garden, M50, and Jing'an

The Yu Garden complex in the Old City is one of Shanghai's busiest tourist draws, so the same logic applies as the Bund: arrive early. The classical Chinese garden at the heart of the complex, built during the Ming dynasty and covering just two hectares, is a masterclass in compressed spatial design — each turn of the path reveals a different composition of rocks, water, pavilions, and plantings. The surrounding bazaar is crowded and commercially intense but worth navigating for the xiaolongbao (soup dumplings) at the original Nanxiang Steamed Bun Restaurant, a Shanghai institution operating from the same location since 1900.

After Yu Garden, head north to M50, the contemporary art district clustered around a former textile mill complex on Moganshan Road. Fifty-plus galleries occupy the industrial spaces, ranging from blue-chip Chinese contemporary art to experimental photography and installation work. Entry to most galleries is free, and the complex itself — red-brick warehouses along a canal — is photographically excellent regardless of what's showing inside. Unlike some "creative districts" that have sanitised themselves into irrelevance, M50 still feels like a working art community rather than a lifestyle concept.

Finish the afternoon in Jing'an, the neighbourhood around Jing'an Temple (the contrast of the ancient gilded temple against the surrounding luxury towers is one of Shanghai's defining images). The streets south of the temple towards Changshu Road have a different character from the French Concession — quieter, more residential, with excellent independent restaurants and some of the city's best bookshops.

## Eating Like a Local

Shanghai's food culture is specific and deeply regional. The city sits at the heart of Jiangnan cuisine, characterised by a preference for sweetness, careful braising, and fresh river seafood — a departure from the spice-forward cooking of Sichuan or Hunan.

- **Xiaolongbao** (soup dumplings) — The version served at Nanxiang in Yu Garden is a benchmark, but neighbourhood dumpling shops throughout the city serve excellent versions for a fraction of the tourist-area price.
- **Shengjianbao** (pan-fried pork buns) — These are to Shanghai what the croissant is to Paris: the essential street breakfast. Crispy-bottomed, juicy, and dangerously hot. Yang's Fry-Dumpling chain is reliable and cheap.
- **Hong shao rou** (red-braised pork belly) — The archetypal Shanghai dish, slow-cooked in soy, sugar, and Shaoxing wine until the fat becomes gelatinous and the meat nearly falls apart. Found on menus throughout the city.
- Hole-in-the-wall noodle shops — Look for the plastic-stool establishments with handwritten menus near metro stations in residential areas. A bowl of scallion oil noodles or beef tendon noodles rarely exceeds ¥25-¥35 and represents the city's everyday food culture at its most unmediated.

## Getting Around Shanghai

Shanghai's metro system is one of the world's most extensive and is the clear first choice for getting around the city. The network covers all major tourist areas, runs frequently throughout the day, and is extremely cheap by international standards — fares rarely exceed ¥6 for any single journey across the city. Signage is in both Chinese and English, and the ticketing machines have English-language options.

Download the Metro Shanghai app before arrival for journey planning. Taxis are plentiful and metered, and DiDi (the Chinese ride-hailing equivalent of Uber) works well for point-to-point trips where the metro would require multiple changes. Traffic in central Shanghai can be significant during rush hours, so factor in extra time if travelling by road between 7-9am and 5-7pm.

## Practical Tips

Visa requirements for Shanghai depend entirely on your passport. Citizens of many countries can access the 144-hour (six-day) transit visa exemption, which allows visa-free entry to Shanghai and surrounding provinces — check the current eligible nationalities list before booking, as it has expanded significantly in recent years.

Shanghai's crowds are intense by any standard, and the major attractions — Yu Garden, the Bund, the French Concession at weekends — can feel overwhelming during peak hours and holiday periods. Weekday mornings are consistently the best time to visit any flagship attraction. Chinese New Year (late January or early February) brings the city to a partial standstill and is best avoided unless you are specifically there for the festival experience.

Mandarin is the working language of Shanghai, though younger residents and those working in tourist-facing industries often speak functional English. Having a translation app installed and your hotel address saved in Chinese characters will resolve the majority of practical communication challenges.
