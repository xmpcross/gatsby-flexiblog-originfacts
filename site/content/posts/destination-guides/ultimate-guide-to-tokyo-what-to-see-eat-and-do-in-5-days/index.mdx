Tokyo is the most intricately layered city on earth. Fourteen million people live within its boundaries, yet it manages to feel navigable, clean, and quietly extraordinary at street level. Five days is enough to understand why travellers return year after year — and enough to see that this city rewards depth over distance. Rather than racing between landmarks, this itinerary takes you through a different face of Tokyo each day, from ancient Asakusa to hypermodern Odaiba, with the neighbourhoods that matter most sitting in between.

If you are working with a tight budget and want to know how to get the most from Tokyo without overspending, pair this itinerary guide with [Tokyo on a Budget: How to Experience the World's Greatest Megacity Without Breaking the Bank](/destination-guides/tokyo-on-a-budget-worlds-greatest-megacity/) for practical money-saving tips on transport, food, and accommodation.

## Day 1: Asakusa and Old Tokyo

Start your first morning before the crowds arrive. Senso-ji Temple in Asakusa is Tokyo's oldest Buddhist temple, and the walk through Kaminarimon gate and along Nakamise shopping street at dawn is one of the most atmospheric experiences the city offers. The incense smoke, the wooden stalls selling ningyo-yaki and roasted senbei, the five-storey pagoda emerging through morning mist — it is the Tokyo of a hundred years ago, still intact.

Spend the rest of the morning in Yanaka, the neighbourhood east of Ueno that survived both the 1923 earthquake and the Second World War firebombing largely intact. The cemetery, the narrow shopping street Yanaka Ginza, the old temples and wooden houses give you a sense of what Shitamachi — old downtown Tokyo — looked and felt like before the city rebuilt itself. Grab a melon pan from a local bakery and take your time.

Afternoon: Ueno Park houses five major museums and a zoo within walking distance of each other. The Tokyo National Museum is the standout, holding the world's largest collection of Japanese art. The park itself is worth an hour, especially the path along Shinobazu Pond. End the day in Akihabara for the sensory contrast it provides: neon signs, multi-storey gadget emporiums, and maid cafes spilling onto the pavement.

## Day 2: Shinjuku and Shibuya

These two districts sit thirty minutes apart by train but represent completely different versions of Tokyo. Start in Shinjuku. The Metropolitan Government Building offers free observation decks on the 45th floor with views across the entire city on clear days. Shinjuku Gyoen National Garden nearby is one of the city's best green spaces, particularly beautiful in cherry blossom season.

Golden Gai deserves an evening return, but walk through in the afternoon to appreciate the scale of what is there: two hundred tiny bars packed into a cluster of alleys the size of a city block, each holding eight to twelve people at most. The density of personality per square metre is unlike anything else in the city.

Take the train to Shibuya for late afternoon. The Scramble Crossing at dusk is as extraordinary as its reputation suggests. Watch from the second floor of the Starbucks opposite for a wider view. Harajuku and Omotesando are a short walk: Takeshita Street for Tokyo's youth fashion subculture, Omotesando for the high-architecture flagships of international luxury brands.

## Day 3: Day Trip to Kamakura

Ninety minutes south of Tokyo by train, Kamakura was Japan's medieval capital and still carries the weight of that history. The Great Buddha — a 13-metre bronze statue sitting open-air in the hills — is the obvious starting point, but the real reward is the hiking trail connecting Kamakura's temples through forested hills above the town.

The Daibutsu hiking course connects Kotoku-in to Kita-Kamakura station through a trail of smaller shrines, bamboo groves, and hilltop viewpoints. Allow three to four hours. The coastal town itself has excellent lunch options, particularly the fresh shirasu bowls served at restaurants near Komachi Street. Return to Tokyo by early evening with time to spare.

## Day 4: West Tokyo and Hidden Neighbourhoods

Shimokitazawa is Tokyo's indie heartland — a neighbourhood of secondhand clothing shops, live music venues, independent coffee roasters, and a resident population of musicians, artists, and students that gives it an energy unlike anywhere else in the city. Browse the vintage shops along the main street, stop for coffee at a record-lined cafe, and look for whatever is playing at one of the dozen small live music venues that open from the afternoon onwards.

From Shimokitazawa, take the train to Nakameguro, particularly pleasant in the afternoon when light hits the canal. The raised walkway along the Meguro River is lined with independent boutiques and cafes. Continue to Daikanyama for the Tsutaya bookshop complex — one of the most beautiful bookshops in the world, spread across three pavilions with a cafe, magazine archive, and curated design section.

## Day 5: Tsukiji, Roppongi, and the City at Night

Begin early at Tsukiji Outer Market. The famous tuna auctions moved to Toyosu, but the outer market remains fully operational and is the best place in Tokyo for a breakfast of fresh sushi or a bowl of seafood rice. Arrive by 7am for the best selection.

Spend the afternoon at one of Tokyo's two great contemporary art museums: the Mori Art Museum in Roppongi Hills or the teamLab digital art installations at Azabudai Hills. Both require advance ticket booking. Roppongi is also home to the National Art Center, which is free to enter and one of the largest gallery spaces in Japan.

End your five days on the water at Odaiba, the artificial island in Tokyo Bay, where the city skyline looks back at you across the water at dusk. The Rainbow Bridge and the illuminated Fuji TV building, the slow ferry back to Hinode Pier — it is a fitting send-off from a city that never quite reveals itself completely, no matter how many days you give it.

## Practical Tips for 5 Days in Tokyo

- Buy a Suica or Pasmo IC card at the airport and load it with 3,000 yen to start — it covers all trains, subways, and buses
- Book teamLab, the Mori Art Museum, and Kamakura day trips in advance, especially in peak season
- Convenience stores are genuinely good for meals — do not overlook them
- Most of the best restaurants do not take reservations; arrive at opening time (typically 11:30am or 5:30pm) to avoid queues
