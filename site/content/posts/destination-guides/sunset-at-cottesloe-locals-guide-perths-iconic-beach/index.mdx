There are beaches, and then there are places that become part of the rhythm of a city's life. Cottesloe Beach is the second kind. Every Perth local has a Cottesloe memory — a summer evening with cold drinks on the grass, a post-work swim in January, a New Year's morning gathering with half the suburb. The beach itself is beautiful in the straightforward way that Indian Ocean beaches tend to be: white sand, turquoise water, and a horizon that stretches uninterrupted all the way to Africa. But what elevates Cottesloe above a simple ranking of great beaches is the ritual. Coming here is something Perth people do, regularly and deliberately, in a way that reveals everything you need to know about how the city understands a good life.

## The Beach Itself

Cottesloe's main swimming area runs for roughly 500 metres between the northern and southern groyne walls. The beach is patrolled by volunteer surf lifesavers on weekends and public holidays, and the designated swimming zones are clearly marked with flags — always swim between the red and yellow flags if you are unfamiliar with the conditions. The water is generally calm and clear within the patrolled zones, making it suitable for families and less confident swimmers.

For those who want something more than a flat-water swim, the **southern groyne** is where the interesting water begins. The groyne extends into the ocean and creates a sheltered area on its northern side where the sand gives way to limestone reef. Snorkelling here rewards the curious: leatherjackets, wrasse, small reef sharks, and occasional octopus are all regular sightings. The visibility on a calm day is remarkable. Surfers, meanwhile, tend to gravitate toward the break at the northern end of the beach during the winter months when swells from the Southern Ocean push up the coast.

The beach has all the facilities you would expect from Perth's most visited stretch of coast: shower blocks, change rooms, a grassed embankment above the sand ideal for picnics, and paid parking within easy walking distance. Arrive by 9am on a summer weekend if you want to secure a parking spot without circling.

## The Sunset Ritual

Perth is one of a small number of cities in the world where the urban beach faces due west over open ocean, and Cottesloe makes the most of this geographical accident every single evening. The sunset ritual here is less an activity and more a civic institution. On any weekday evening in summer, the grassed area above the beach fills steadily from about 6pm as people arrive with bottles of wine, BYO food, and a general commitment to doing nothing more demanding than watching the sky change colour.

The best spots to watch are from the upper grass terraces rather than the beach itself — the slightly elevated position frames the water and horizon cleanly, and you avoid the sun sitting directly in your eyes during the final hour before dusk. Locals tend to plant themselves along the low retaining wall that runs parallel to Marine Parade, or spread out on the lawn in front of the Indiana Tea House. Arrive at least 45 minutes before sunset to secure a good position in summer. The horizon turns deep orange, then red, then briefly a vivid pink before the light drops — on a clear evening the whole sequence takes about 20 minutes and is worth every minute of patience it demands.

## Where to Eat and Drink Near Cottesloe

The eating and drinking options around Cottesloe are genuinely good, which is a significant part of why the sunset ritual sustains itself so reliably.

**Indiana Tea House** is the landmark. Built in a sprawling colonial bungalow style directly above the beach, Indiana has been a fixture at Cottesloe for well over a century in various forms. The current incarnation serves a menu of contemporary Australian food across a main dining room and a large veranda that looks directly over the beach. It is busy, moderately expensive, and worth booking ahead for weekend evenings. Even if you are not eating a full meal, the bar is a perfectly reasonable place to watch the sunset with a gin and tonic.

**Blue Duck** sits on the main road just back from the beach and operates as a relaxed café by day and a more animated bar and restaurant by night. The food leans toward fresh and seasonal, the coffee is excellent, and the staff are reliably friendly. It is a good choice for breakfast before a morning swim, particularly if Indiana's more formal setting doesn't appeal.

For something simpler and entirely in keeping with the beach experience, the **fish and chips** options along Marine Parade are an institution of their own. Wrapping up a parcel of fresh flathead and chips and eating on the grass above the beach as the sun drops is exactly the kind of activity Cottesloe seems designed to enable. It is unhurried, inexpensive, and completely satisfying.

## Sculptures by the Sea

Every March, Cottesloe hosts **Sculptures by the Sea**, a free outdoor exhibition that transforms the beach and surrounding landscape into one of the most unusual art spaces in the Southern Hemisphere. Over 70 sculptors from Australia and around the world install large-scale works in the sand, on the groyne walls, and along the grassed terraces above the beach. The exhibition runs for about 12 days and attracts tens of thousands of visitors.

The combination of sea air, white sand, and ambitious outdoor sculpture makes for a genuinely memorable experience, and the scale of the works — many designed specifically to interact with the tidal environment — goes well beyond what most outdoor art events attempt. If your visit coincides with March, arrange your schedule around it. Arrive early on a weekday morning for the best light and the thinnest crowds. ## Getting There from Perth CBD

Cottesloe is well connected to the city centre and requires no car.

- **By train:** The Fremantle line stops at Cottesloe Station, which is a flat 10-minute walk from the beach. Trains run frequently from Perth Station throughout the day and into the late evening. The journey takes around 20 minutes from the city centre.
- **By bus:** Several bus routes service the Cottesloe area, connecting to the train network and to nearby suburbs including Claremont and Nedlands.
- **By bike:** The coastal cycling path connects Cottesloe to Fremantle to the south and Scarborough to the north, making it an attractive destination for cyclists who want to combine a ride with a swim.

Driving is possible but not necessary. Parking fills quickly on weekends and summer evenings — if you are planning to arrive around sunset on a Saturday, allow extra time or take the train. The walk from the station is straightforward, flat, and takes you through a pleasant residential streetscape of Federation-era houses and Norfolk pines.

Cottesloe is one of those rare places that lives up to its reputation entirely on its own terms. It does not try to be dramatic or exclusive — it simply offers a beach, a view, and a sunset that Perth people have been returning to for generations.
